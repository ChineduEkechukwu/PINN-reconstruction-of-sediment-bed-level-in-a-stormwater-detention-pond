# PINN-reconstruction-of-sediment-bed-level-in-a-stormwater-detention-pond
Physics-informed neural network (PINN) reconstructing sediment bed-level change in a nature-based stormwater detention pond, River Chew catchment (UK). Fuses cheap dual-temperature sensors (Luce–Tonina Δz) with GNSS levelling via a thermoelastic kernel. Includes baselines, Monte Carlo uncertainty, and full reproducible pipeline.
## Stage detail
A — Data preparation. Renames sensor columns, parses dayfirst=True, groups by calendar day, and fits one diurnal harmonic per day per sensor by least squares. Amplitude A = √(a₁²+b₁²), phase φ = atan2(−b₁, a₁) wrapped to [0, 2π). Phase difference wrapped to [−π, π].
B — LT Delta solver. Applies the Luce–Tonina formulation to the daily harmonics. Guards: amplitude floor min_amp=1e-9, phase floor min_dphi=1e-9 (→ NaN), η clipped to ±30. Optional centred rolling smoothing via smooth_days (default 0 = off). Statistics printed here are what motivated the outlier correction.
S1 — Baselines (no PyTorch). - B1 LT-direct — cumulative sum of the daily LT Δz; the conventional estimator’s own quantitative claim, plus a 30-day rolling mean for plotting. - B2 Crank–Nicolson — sparse implicit FD solution of the same heat equation at literature κ = 5e-7 m² s⁻¹, BCs T(0,t)=T_LT(t) and T(z_max,t)=mean(T_LT), IC T(z,0)=T_LT(0); α calibrated offline against the GNSS endpoint (α_fd ≈ −8.2e-3). - B4 GP kriging — ConstantKernel × RBF + WhiteKernel on the 104 GNSS points with full leave-one-out CV. Uses no thermal information; it is the spatial reference.
S2 — Main PINN + B3. - Inputs (z/z_max, t_norm): 4 hidden layers × 32 tanh neurons → T in °C. Xavier init, zero biases. - κ and α are learnable scalars, sigmoid-remapped into κ ∈ [1e-7, 1e-6] m² s⁻¹ and α ∈ [−0.5, 0.5] K⁻¹ (signed, init −1e-2). - 5,000 Latin-hypercube collocation points (pure-NumPy LHS), split at the same 80/20 temporal boundary as the observations (train days 0:254, val 254:318). - Composite loss (Eq. 12): 1.0·PDE + 10·LT + 20·GNSS + 10·BC + 5·IC. The PDE residual is scaled by period_days and uses κ_day = κ·86400 so the equation is dimensionally correct in physical units throughout. - Thermoelastic kernel: u(t) = α · ∫₀^{z_max} (T(z,t) − T_REF) dz, 100-point trapezoid, no ad-hoc depth weighting. - Optimisation: 3,000 Adam epochs with early stopping on held-out LT-surface MSE (patience 8), then up to 500 L-BFGS iterations. - B3 DataMLP — identical architecture and GNSS/LT losses, PDE/BC/IC omitted. The only difference is the physics, which is exactly what makes the RMSE gap (2.17 vs 2.63 °C) interpretable as the physics residual’s contribution.
S3 — Uncertainty and spatial skill. - MC ensemble (N=50) — samples κ log-normally and z_max normally, warm-starts every member from one shared 500-epoch ensemble baseline, fine-tunes 150 Adam steps each on reduced collocation. Yields the envelope, percentiles, CV, and decision horizon. - LOO-CV PINN — holds out one GNSS point at a time and refits against the spatial mean of the remaining 103, on a stratified 20-point subset spanning the dz_gnss range (compute-bound). All 104 points are covered by GP kriging for the headline comparison. The analytic extrapolation to all 104 gives 82.5 mm vs GP’s 55.6 mm.
S4 — Figures. Matplotlib (Agg) + Plotly. Fixed palette (#185FA5, #534AB7, #1D9E75, #D85A30, #888780, #BA7517). Figures 5 (temporal reconstruction + loss curves), 6 (depth-lag vs Stallman), 7 (GNSS map + LOO residual maps, GP vs PINN), 8 (probe-location sediment behaviour), 9 (MC envelope), 10 (MC diagnostics), 11 (4-panel baseline comparison), 12–13 (supporting).
## Reuse and extension
This pipeline is deliberately small, CPU-only, and built from two cheap temperature sensors plus two survey epochs — the point is transferability to other low-instrumentation nature-based assets. Common adaptations:
Apply to a different pond. Replace temperature_sensors.csv (same four columns, day-first timestamps) and GNSS.xlsx (same six columns). Then check, in order: Z_MAX (column depth) and Z_SENSOR (burial depth) in common.py; the T_BASE / DELTA_T envelope anchor in load_data(), which is calibrated to British pond conditions and will not transfer unexamined; and ke in compute_delta_z_LuceTonina().
Change the physics. pde_residual() in common.py is the single place the 1-D heat equation lives. thermo_u() is the single place the thermoelastic kernel lives. Both take model and are used identically by the PINN, the MC members, and (for the kernel) the B2 FD baseline — so a change propagates consistently across the comparison.
Reweight the loss. LAMBDA_PDE / LT / GNSS / BC / IC are module-level constants. The current GNSS weight (20) is the highest because the survey endpoint is the only absolute anchor in the system; lowering it lets the surface fit dominate and α drifts.
Widen the parameter bounds. KAPPA_MIN/MAX and ALPHA_MIN/MAX define the sigmoid remapping. Note the caveat above: the loss surface is flat below ≈10⁻⁷ m² s⁻¹, so lowering KAPPA_MIN will not sharpen the estimate — it will widen an already unidentified bound.
Scale the uncertainty analysis. N_MC (50), ENS_EPOCHS (500), MC_EPOCHS (150), and the κ/z_max sampling distributions are all in S3. The reported CV of 0.87 % is narrow given the warm-start protocol — members share one baseline and fine-tune briefly, so the envelope reflects (κ, z_max) sensitivity around a common optimum, not full retraining spread. Raise MC_EPOCHS if you want the looser, more conservative envelope.
Extend LOO-CV to all 104 points. S3 runs a stratified 20-point subset for compute. Set n_loo to the full set for a like-for-like comparison with GP kriging’s 104-point LOO RMSE.
The obvious open extension. The PINN currently has no spatial dimension, which is precisely why GP kriging beats it per-point. Extending the network input from (z, t) to (x, y, z, t) and fitting against per-point dz_gnss rather than the spatial mean is the natural next step — the GNSS file already carries the eastings and northings.
